Read the water first

If the water looks chaotic, do not force it. The right first lesson day is simple: clean lines, manageable size, and an instructor who moves you to the correct spot. Your win condition is not standing for Instagram. Your win is control.

Green flags (go)

You want a day where you can repeat the basics without getting worked.

  • Small to medium waves you can paddle into.
  • Clear lineup zones with space to learn.
  • Instructor is specific, not hype.

Red flags (adjust)

If these are present, still do the lesson, but ask to shift location or shorten the session.

  • Strong currents pulling you sideways.
  • Crowded lineup with experienced surfers only.
  • Sharp reef exposure at low tide.

Lesson blueprint

Three stages. Keep it simple.
1

Get setup right

Warm up, learn board position, and practice pop up on sand. This is where the “stand up” gets built correctly.

2

Control before standing

In the water you learn paddling rhythm, how to angle the board, and how to safely fall. No ego, no shortcuts.

3

One clean ride

Pick one simple target: one controlled takeoff, one stable stance, then exit safely. Repeat. That is the real progress.

Safety rules

  • Never dive headfirst. Protect your neck.
  • Fall flat, away from the board.
  • If you lose the board, cover your head.
  • Listen to your instructor on where to paddle.

Comfort rules

  • Drink water before and after. You will cramp if you do not.
  • Use reef safe sunscreen and reapply.
  • Rash guard is not optional. It saves your skin.
  • Stop early if you are gassed. You will learn faster next session.

Common beginner mistake

Trying to stand too early, then getting slammed, then calling it “hard.” Slow it down. Get the setup right. Repeat basics until it clicks.

Pick the right instructor

You want clear coaching, not noise. A good instructor fixes your stance, your timing, and your panic. If they cannot explain what to do with your hands and feet, find another one.

Ask: where will we paddle?
Ask: what is the one goal today?

Gear you actually need

For a first lesson, keep it basic. Most setups are included. Your job is to show up protected and hydrated.

Bring: rash guard, water, cash
Skip: valuables, heavy bags

FAQ

Where should I take my first surf lesson in Siargao?

Cloud 9 and the nearby beginner zones (often called Jacking Horse) are the usual starting points. Your instructor will pick the safest spot based on conditions.

How long should my first session be?

60 to 120 minutes is the sweet spot. Long enough to learn the sequence, short enough to keep form from collapsing.

Is reef a real concern?

Yes. Ask about tide and entry points. Follow your instructor’s fall and exit guidance. Do not freestyle it.

What should I do after the lesson?

Hydrate, stretch your shoulders, and do a short debrief. If you enjoyed it, book session two while the muscle memory is fresh.
